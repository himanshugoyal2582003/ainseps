# Chapter 7: Results

The results chapter details the empirical outcomes generated by the AI-Based Stock Price Trend Prediction System. It evaluates the effectiveness of the hybrid machine learning architecture (LSTM + XGBoost) in analyzing historical equity data and accurately forecasting future trajectories. The system's performance is assessed through quantitative metrics and qualitative graphical analysis.

## 7.1 Output of the System

The primary output of the system is a rich, JSON-formatted data payload delivered via the FastAPI backend to the Next.js frontend. This payload contains:
- **Historical Baselines:** The actual recorded closing prices of the queried stock over a defined lookback period.
- **Predicted Trajectories:** A forecasted sequence of future stock price values (e.g., predicting the next 30 trading days).
- **Technical Indicators:** Computed values for MACD, RSI, and moving averages corresponding to the historical data.

The frontend consumes this payload and renders a highly interactive dashboard.

## 7.2 Graphical Representation

The most immediate and intuitive method for evaluating the model's performance is through visual comparison. The system utilizes `Recharts` to generate dynamic line graphs overlaying the model's predictions on top of actual hold-out test data (during the validation phase) and projecting into the unknown future (during live inference).

- **Training Fit Visualization:** During the backtesting phase, the system plots the model's historical predictions against the actual historical data. A tightly tracking line indicates that the LSTM has successfully learned the fundamental variance of the stock.
- **Future Projection Visualization:** The actual, historical stock prices are rendered as a solid line. The predicted future stock prices are rendered as a distinctly colored, dashed line extending beyond the current date.
- **Confidence Intervals:** When applicable, the system displays a shaded region around the prediction line, representing the model's confidence interval. A wider shaded area indicates higher market volatility and lower predictive certainty.

The visual comparison immediately highlights the model's capacity to follow general momentum trends, though it visually confirms that extreme, single-day spikes (noise) are generally smoothed over by the algorithm.

## 7.3 Performance Analysis and Metrics

To objectively evaluate the predictive accuracy of the Machine Learning models, standard regression metrics are calculated during the testing phase, comparing the model's predicted values ($\hat{y}$) against the actual true values ($y$).

1. **Root Mean Square Error (RMSE):** 
   RMSE calculates the standard deviation of the prediction errors (residuals). Because the errors are squared before averaging, RMSE heavily penalizes large errors. 
   - A lower RMSE value indicates that the predicted stock prices are, on average, closer to the actual prices. 
   - *Observation:* The LSTM model generally achieved a lower RMSE than baseline linear regression models, demonstrating its superior ability to handle non-linear time-series data.

2. **Mean Absolute Error (MAE):**
   MAE calculates the average absolute distance between the predicted values and the actual values. Unlike RMSE, it treats all errors equally, providing a straightforward, interpretable metric of how far off the prediction was in actual currency terms (e.g., "The model was off by an average of ₹15 per share").

3. **Directional Accuracy:**
   In finance, predicting the exact price is often less important than predicting the correct direction (up or down). The system measures the percentage of days where the model correctly forecasted the sign of the price movement, regardless of the magnitude.

## 7.4 Observations

Rigorous testing of the system across various ticker symbols (e.g., highly liquid large-cap stocks vs. volatile small-cap stocks) yielded several critical observations:

- **Trend Identification:** The hybrid model performs exceptionally well at identifying broad, macro-level trends. If a stock enters a prolonged 6-month bullish channel, the model successfully recognizes the momentum and forecasts continued upward trajectories.
- **Smoothing of Volatility:** Both the LSTM and XGBoost models tend to output "smoothed" predictions. They struggle to predict extreme, single-day gaps (such as a 10% drop following a disastrous earnings report) because these events are statistically anomalous and not predictably represented in preceding price action.
- **Short-Term vs. Long-Term:** Prediction accuracy degrades significantly as the forecasting horizon increases. Predicting the price $T+1$ (tomorrow) yields a much lower error rate than predicting $T+30$ (one month from now). The compounding nature of market uncertainty makes long-term point-estimates highly unreliable.

## 7.5 Limitations of Results

While the system represents a sophisticated application of AI, the results are constrained by inherent limitations in the data and the fundamental nature of financial markets.

1. **The Efficient Market Hypothesis:** Markets react instantaneously to new information. Because this model relies exclusively on historical price action and technical indicators, it cannot anticipate external "Black Swan" events. A sudden geopolitical conflict, a global pandemic, or an unexpected CEO resignation will instantly invalidate the model's predictions.
2. **Lack of Fundamental Context:** In its current iteration, the model does not ingest fundamental financial statements (P/E ratios, debt loads, revenue growth). It predicts purely based on price psychology, meaning it may bullishly predict a failing company simply because it experienced a short-term short-squeeze.
3. **Overfitting Risks:** Despite implementing dropout layers in the LSTM and regularization in XGBoost, financial time-series models are perennially at risk of overfitting to historical noise rather than true signal, particularly in non-stationary markets.

## 7.6 Summary of Results

The project successfully demonstrates the powerful application of full-stack web development and machine learning in the domain of financial time-series forecasting. The integration of FastAPI and Next.js proved to be an exceptionally robust architecture for serving computationally heavy data science models to an end-user in real-time.

While the predictive accuracy of the models proves that historical price patterns contain extractable signal, the limitations emphatically underscore a core tenet of quantitative finance: AI models are highly sophisticated tools for risk assessment and trend analysis, but they cannot eliminate the fundamental, stochastic uncertainty inherent in the stock market.
